# Silver Lifestyle Wellness Overlay

## Use when

Use for older-adult lifestyle, comfort, mobility-adjacent, convenience, sleep-environment, daily care, family gifting, or non-medical wellness products.

## Demand and trust

- The buyer may be the user, spouse, adult child, caregiver, or gift buyer; each has different needs and authority.
- Trust depends on dignity, ease of use, legibility, service, realistic benefit, safety, and family confidence.
- Avoid framing older consumers only through fear, decline, or medicalized stereotypes.

## Product and evidence

- Record physical/cognitive demands, setup, controls, readability, grip, maintenance, compatibility, supervision, and service requirements.
- Demonstrate actual use and limitations with representative users where appropriate.

## Commercial risks

- Education, service, installation, returns, replacement parts, bulky logistics, and family decision cycles can raise acquisition and support cost.
- Gifting may produce seasonal spikes but weak direct-user activation.

## Operations and supply risks

- Monitor usability complaints, setup failure, delivery/installation, packaging accessibility, spare parts, warranty, and escalation for safety incidents.
- Customer service should use clear language and avoid speculative health advice.

## Compliance boundaries

- Do not imply diagnosis, treatment, prevention, restored function, guaranteed sleep, or medical outcomes without proper status and evidence.
- Distinguish comfort/convenience products from regulated medical or health products.

## Lifecycle

- Model household adoption, caregiver involvement, habit formation, replacement/service cycle, gifting, and changing needs.

## Specialist application

Product must eventually own accessibility and safety requirements; Marketing must preserve dignity; Operations must provide patient service; Analytics must separate buyer and user outcomes.
